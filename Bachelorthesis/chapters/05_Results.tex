\chapter{Results and Discussion}
\label{ch:results}
The abovementioned measurement optimizations were implemented and measurements were conducted on two samples with Nb layer thicknesses of $d_\textrm{Nb} = \SI{45}{nm}$ and $d_\textrm{Nb} = \SI{30}{nm}$. Two distinct sets of measurements were performed on each sample:
One by Max Mangold in November 2024 (Series A) and another by Alex Burg in May 2025 (Series B). The measurements conducted by Max Mangold served as a reference point, while the measurements performed by Alex Burg incorporated the optimizations discussed in chapter \ref{ch:FullFilmMagneticResonance} and focused especially on the area near the critical temperature $T_\textrm{C}$. In the following, the results from both measurement series will be compared and discussed.
\section{Results of sample with $d_\textrm{Nb} = \SI{45}{nm}$}
Figure \ref{fig:45nm_params} shows extracted parameters $\alpha$, $\Delta H_0$,$M_\textrm{eff}$ and $H_{\text{shift}}$ of $d_\textrm{Nb} = \SI{45}{nm}$.

For both series, the Gilbert damping parameter $\alpha$ increases with temperature until around the critical temperature $T_{\textrm{C}}$. This behavior can be explained by the fact that the London penetration depth is dependent on temperature: $\lambda(T) = \lambda(0) / \sqrt{1 - (T/T_C)^4}$. This means that more magnetic moment is able to transfer into the superconductor when the temperature is increased, which results in a stronger damping of the magnetization. Above $T_{\textrm{C}}$, the damping becomes constant since the supercondutor transitions into a normal state. There is a significant spike visible in both series near $T_\textrm{C}$, although the spike seems to be larger for series B.

The resonance line width offset $\Delta H_0$ decreases with increase in temperature, due to fewer extrinsic factors being present. Jeon et al. explained this by the fact that when $d_\textrm{Nb}$ is close to the London penetration depth $\lambda_\textrm{L}$, the superconducting layer perturbs the magnetization of the Permalloy and cause an incoherent precession near the interface \cite{Jeon2019}. This leads to a broadening of the resonance line. After the downward spike near $T_{\textrm{C}}$, the offset becomes close to zero, which is logical since the superconductor is in a normal state and thus no extrinsic factors are present.

The effective magnetization $M_\textrm{eff}$ appears to exhibit two distinct constant levels: one below $T_\textrm{C}$ and one above, with the higher value occurring above $T_\textrm{C}$. Similar to the other parameters, a pronounced change is observed in the vicinity of $T_\textrm{C}$. The direction of this change differs between series A and B. The plot of series A only has one data point near $T_\textrm{C}$, which makes it difficult to draw definitive conclusions. Therefore, the data points from series B are considered more reliable.

The resonance field shift $H_{\text{shift}}$ decreases with increasing temperature, which can be explained by the fact that the superconductor is less able to expel magnetic field lines, since the newly created vortices allow magnetic fields to penetrate into the superconductor. This results in flux focusing becoming weaker, explaining the decrease in the resonance field shift. Above $T_{\textrm{C}}$, the resonance field shift becomes zero, as expected. Since $H_\textrm{shift}$ has, in theory, the same contribution as an anisotropy field, this also means that our sample is isotropic above $T_\textrm{C}$, as expected from Permalloy.

\section{Result of sample with $d_\textrm{Nb} = \SI{30}{nm}$}

For $d_\textrm{Nb} = \SI{30}{nm}$, the Gilbert damping parameter $\alpha$ shows a significant downward offset in the measurements done by Alex Burg compared to those done by Max Mangold, even though the underlying trends appear to be similar. It should be noted that the huge spike is only present in the series B, because the data point near $T_{\textrm{C}}$ in series A was too bad to be fitted properly.
The overall trend seems to be consistent with the measurement done on the 45 nm sample, with an increase in damping below $T_{\text{C}}$, followed by a spike and a constant value above $T_{\text{C}}$.

The resonance line width offset $\Delta H_0$, on the contrary, shows a strong alignment between the two measurements done. The downward spike is also only visible on the data points of series B. Here, the trend also seems to be consistent with the 45 nm sample.

The effective magnetization seems to exhibit a large discrepancy between series A and B. While series A shows an increase of the magnetization until $T_{\text{C}}$, followed by a constant value, the data points of series B seem to show no constant behavior and seem to have a significant offset upwards of around 15 mT. This could potentially be caused by sample aging, leading to mutual interdiffusion of Permalloy and Nb layers, altering the effective magnetization.

The resonance field shift $H_{\text{shift}}$ also shows alignment between the two measurements, although it can be noted here that there is no significant spike visible on either of the measurements. Additionally, $H_{\text{shift}}$ seemes to settle at a non-zero constant value above $T_{\text{C}}$, which is unexpected. It suggests unexpected anisotropy in the Permalloy, which was not pursued further in this thesis.

\section{Discussion}
This thesis was partly inspired by the work of Jeon et al., particularly their article attempting to explain the increase in spin pumping below the superconducting transition temperature with a superconducting pure spin current \cite{Jeon2018}. In this article, Jeon et al. have observed an increase in the resonance linewidth $\Delta H$ below the critical temperature for samples with a Nb thickness of 15 nm and 30 nm. Since quasiparticle interactions freeze out at lower temperatures, they argued that the spin current increase can only explained by spin-triplet spincurrents. However, this experiment was only done on a fixed microwave frequency of $f = 20 \ \textrm{GHz}$. This limitation makes it difficult to directly investigate the Gilbert-damping $\alpha$, as $\alpha$ can be best assessed by measuring the slope of the frequency-dependent resonance linewidth (see Eq. (\ref{linewidth equation})). Müller et al. \cite{PhysRevLett.126.087201} and Mangold (unpublished) conducted broadband FMR measurements and found no increase in Gilbert damping below $T_\textrm{C}$, yielding no evidence for spin-triplet supercurrents. Also, samples measured in this thesis also show no increase in $\alpha$ below $T_\textrm{C}$, but rather a decrease, which is consistent with the findings of Müller et al. and Mangold.

A subsequent article by Jeon et al. from 2019 on Permalloy films sandwiched between two Nb layers (100nm) improves upon this and employs methods similar to those used in this thesis, involving frequency-dependent measurements to investigate the Gilbert-damping \cite{Jeon2019}. In the article, the resonance curve is measured for frequencies from 5-20 GHz for temperatures from 2-80 K and the resulting four parameters ($\alpha$, $\Delta H_0$, $M_\textrm{eff}$, $H_\textrm{shift}$) were extracted. In general, the trends observed in their article are mirrored in this thesis, especially when compared to the result for the 45 nm sample.

The researchers observed a similar drop in the Gilbert-damping parameter $\alpha$ from around 0.016 above $T_\textrm{C}$ to 0.0011 at 2 K. This trend closely aligns with the measurements for the 45 nm sample in this thesis (see \ref{fig:45nm_params}), although the absolute values of $\alpha$ are slightly lower in this thesis. The 30 nm sample shows the same trend as well, with a much smaller drop from around 0.01 to around 0.008.

For the resonance offset $\Delta H_0$, a significant increase is visible from around 0 mT near $T_\textrm{C}$ to reaching a maximum at 4 K at slightly above 6 mT and dropping down to 4 mT for 2 K. This increasing trend can be seen on both our samples as well, although at a much smaller scale. The 45 nm sample only has an increase from around 0 mT to 1 mT, while the 30 nm sample has an increase from around 0 mT to 0.04 mT. The absolute values of $\Delta H_0$ are thus significantly smaller in our samples.

The resonance shift $H_\textrm{shift}$ decreases from 0 mT to around -50 mT at 2 K. This trend is also mirrored in the measurements done in this thesis, although the absolute values are again smaller, with the 45 nm sample showing a shift of around -10 mT and the 30 nm sample around -13 mT.

The differences can be attributed to the difference in thickness between the samples used in the article of Jeon et al. and this thesis. A sample with a Nb layer of 100 nm would demonstrate significantly stronger superconducting properties, which would explain the larger change in $\alpha$, $\Delta H_0$ and $H_\textrm{shift}$.

The results of the study done by Jeon et al. \cite{Jeon2019} thus is confirmed by the results obtained in this thesis, especially for the 45 nm sample. 

The spiking behavior near $T_{\textrm{C}}$ for all parameters can potentially be attributed to domains building near $T_{\textrm{C}}$ in the supercondutor, where some domains are superconducting and others are not. An alternative explanation could be that the spike is caused by vortices that are pinned onto defects under $T_\textrm{C}$ suddenly having enough thermal energy to break free and move around. Both explanations lead to a disturbance in the spin-transfer mechanism. This disturbance leads to an incoherent precession of the magnetization, leading to a broadening of the resonance line and a weaker signal. This spiking behavior has not been reported yet, and displays interesting physics that could be investigated further in future studies.
