\section{Fundamentals of Magnetism}
\subsection{Macrospin Model}
The magnetic properties of a ferromagnet can be best described by treating the ferromagnet as having one average magnetic dipole. This average dipole, called Magnetization $\boldsymbol{M}$, can then be calculated by the sum of all dipole moments $\mu$ of a sample:
\begin{equation} 
\boldsymbol{M} = \frac{1}{V}\sum_{i\in V}{\boldsymbol{\mu_i}}
\end{equation}
In a magnetized medium, the magnetic flux density takes on the following form \cite{obstbaum2014}:
\begin{equation} 
\textbf{B} = \mu_0(\textbf{H+M})
\end{equation}
where $\textbf{H}$ is the magnetic field applied.

From classical electrodynamics, we know that the magnetic dipoles are related to the angular momentum $\textbf{L}$ by the gyromagnetic ratio $\gamma$, due to the electron creating a current through its orbital path:
\begin{equation}
   \boldsymbol{\mu} = \gamma \textbf{L} 
   \label{Drehimpulsgleichung}
\end{equation}
Furthermore, when an external field is applied to the medium, it causes a torque $\textbf{T}$ on the medium:
\begin{equation}
    \textbf{T} = \boldsymbol{\mu} \times \textbf{B}
    \label{Drehmoment von dipolmoment}
\end{equation}
By using equations \ref{Drehimpulsgleichung} and \ref{Drehmoment von dipolmoment}, as well as the fact that $\textbf{T} = \diff{\textbf{L}}{t}$, we get the following equation of motion for a dipole moment $\boldsymbol{\mu}$:
\begin{equation}
    \diff{\boldsymbol{\mu}}{t} = \gamma \boldsymbol{\mu} \times \textbf{B} 
    \label{EOM fuer dipolmoment}
\end{equation}
To transfer this result to the dynamics of the magnetization of a medium, equation (\ref{EOM fuer dipolmoment}) has to be reformulated in such a way that that accounts for the effective field $\boldsymbol{{H_{\text{eff}}}}$ inside the medium. Doing this, we get the Landau-Lifschitz equation \cite{obstbaum2014}:
\begin{equation}
    \diff{\textbf{M}}{t} = - \gamma \textbf{M} \times \mu_0 \boldsymbol{{H_{\text{eff}}}}
    \label{Landau-Lifschitz}
\end{equation}
Here, $\boldsymbol{{H_{\text{eff}}}}$ combines all possible field terms inside the medium, like anisotropy, exchange interaction, and the demagnetizing field \cite{obstbaum2014}.
Solving this differential equation leads to a magnetization undergoing precession around the effective field with the frequency $ \omega = \gamma \mu_0 H_{\text{eff}}$ \cite{Mueller2019}, as shown in figure \ref{fig:precession picture}.

The Landau-Lifschitz equation describes a perpetual precession around the effective magnetic field. In a real physical system, the precession amplitude declines due to various dissipation effects such as magnon-phonon relaxation or radiative damping. Gilbert therefore introduced a damping term with the coefficient $\alpha$ that is material-dependent, with which we get the Landau-Lifschitz-Gilbert (LLG) equation \cite{Liensberger2021}
\begin{equation}
    \diff{\textbf{M}}{t} = - \gamma \textbf{M} \times \mu_0 \boldsymbol{{H_{\text{eff}}}} + \frac{\alpha}{M_s}\boldsymbol{M} \times\diff{\textbf{M}}{t}
\end{equation}
where $M_s$ refers to the saturation magnetization. 

\subsection{Ferromagnetic Resonance}
In order to investigate the resonance condition of this precession, we introduce an oscillating magnetic field $\boldsymbol{h_{rf}}$ that is perpendicular to $\boldsymbol{{H_{\text{eff}}}}$. This oscillating magnetic field can generate a torque that can compensate the damping of the precession when the frequencies match, which can be compared to a driven harmonic oscillator in the classical view \cite{Liensberger2021}.


The following derivation will closely follow the derivations done in \cite{Mueller2019} and \cite{Liensberger2017}. We choose our coordinate system so that $\boldsymbol{{H_{\text{eff}}}}$ lies in the z-axis and $\boldsymbol{h_{rf}}$ to be in the x-y plane. We split $\textbf{H}$ and $\textbf{M}$ into their respective time-independent and time-dependent parts,
\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
        \mathbf{H}_{\text{eff}} &= \mathbf{H}_{\text{ext}} - \mathbf{H}_{\text{de}} + \mathbf{h}_{\text{rf}}(t) \\
        &= (H_{\text{ext}} - N_z M_0) \mathbf{\hat{e}}_z - N_x m_x(t) \mathbf{\hat{e}}_x - N_y m_y(t) \mathbf{\hat{e}}_y + \mathbf{h}_{\text{rf}}(t) \\
        \mathbf{M} &= \mathbf{M}_0 + \mathbf{m}(t) = M_0 \mathbf{\hat{e}}_z + \mathbf{m}(t)
    \end{aligned}
    \label{time-dependent and independent}
\end{equation}
where $N_{x/y/z}$ denote the terms from the demagnetization tensor. For simplicity, the anisotropy field is neglected.
For the oscillating magnetic field $\boldsymbol{h_{rf}}$ and $\textbf{m}(t)$, we use a harmonic ansatz:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{h}_{\text{rf}}(t) &= (h_{\text{rf},x}, h_{\text{rf},y}, 0)^{\text{T}} \cdot e^{i\omega t} \\
\mathbf{m}(t) &= (m_{x}, m_{y}, 0)^{\text{T}} \cdot e^{i\omega t}
\label{harmonic ansatz}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
By plugging in equations \ref{time-dependent and independent} and \ref{harmonic ansatz} into the LLG-equation, and through various steps, we can derive the formula for the resonance frequency $\omega_\text{res}$
\begin{equation}
\omega_\text{res} = \gamma\mu_0 \sqrt{\left[H_{\text{ext}} + (N_x - N_z) \cdot M_s\right]\left[H_{\text{ext}} + (N_y - N_z) \cdot M_s\right]}
\label{Kittel equation}
\end{equation}
which is also known as the Kittel equation. If the magnetic field $H_{\text{ext}}$ is applied in the plane of the sample (ip), the equation can be further simplified to:
\begin{equation}
\omega_\text{res} = \gamma \mu_0 \sqrt{H_{\text{ext}}(H_{\text{ext}}+M_\text{s})}
\end{equation}
Additionally, the Kittel formula gives us an equation for the resonance line width $\Delta H$, as a function of the driving-frequency and the damping coefficient \cite{Mueller2023}:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta H_{FWHM} = 2 \frac{\alpha \omega}{\mu_0 \gamma}
    \label{linewidth equation}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha$ is the Gilbert-damping coefficient and $\Delta H_{FWHM}$ the line width defined as the full-width at half-maximum.

When a ferromagnetic film comes in contact with a normal metal, an increase in damping is observed, which is known as spin pumping. Theoretically, this can be explained by a pure spin current $\boldsymbol{J_\textbf{s}}$ that gets sent across the ferromagnet/metal interface which transports angular momentum from the ferromagnet to the normal metal, hence increasing the damping. The spin current density is given by 
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{J}_{\mathbf{s}}=\frac{\hbar}{4 \pi}\left(\operatorname{Re}\left(G_{\uparrow \downarrow}\right) \mathbf{M} \times \frac{d \mathbf{M}}{d t}-\operatorname{Im}\left(G_{\uparrow \downarrow}\right) \frac{d \mathbf{M}}{d t}\right)
\end{equation}
where $G_{\uparrow \downarrow}$ is the spin-mixing conductance that describes the transport of spins that are not collinear to the magnetization of the sample \cite{Mueller2023}. The first term corresponds to a damping-like behavior, and the second term gives a field-like contribution. The imaginary part of the spin-mixing conductance is typically very small to be negligible ~\cite{obstbaum2014}. The additional contribution in damping is then given by 
\begin{align*}
    \alpha_{sp} =& \ \frac{g\mu_0}{4 \pi M_S}\frac{G_{\uparrow \downarrow}}{d_{\text{FM}}} \\
    \alpha =& \ \alpha_0 + \alpha_{sp}
\end{align*}
where $\alpha_0$ is the Gilbert-damping coefficient with just the ferromagnet and $g$ is the Landé-Factor. \section{Fundamentals of Superconductivity}
In this section, the fundamental theory behind superconductivity is discussed that is needed for the discussion of the results given in chapter 5-6. 

Superconductors are materials that exhibit two distinct properties below a certain temperature: The vanishing of electrical resistance and the expulsion of the magnetic field inside the material, also called the Meißner effect. In its essence, superconductivity is caused by a positive attraction of two electrons through an exchange of virtual phonons. Intuitively, this can be explained by an electron moving through the crystal, causing a deformation in the lattice that generates a positive dipole moment, which in turns attracts another electron. These so-called Cooper-pairs have opposite momentum $\boldsymbol{k}$ and $\textbf{-k}$ \cite{Hunklinger2018}. 
In 1935, two brothers developed the London-equations that attempted to explain the behavior of superconductors:
\begin{align}
    \frac{\partial\boldsymbol{j_s}}{\partial t} \ =& \  \frac{ne^2}{m}\textbf{E} \\
    \nabla \times \boldsymbol{j_s} \ =& \ -\frac{ne^2}{m}\textbf{B}
\end{align}
where $n$ represents the Cooper-pair density and $m$ the mass of a Cooper-pair.
Solving these equations yields 
\begin{align}
    \textbf{B(}\textbf{r}) =& \boldsymbol{B_0}e^{-\frac{|r|}{\lambda_L}}
\end{align}
, where $\lambda_L$ = $\frac{m}{\mu_0 ne^2}$ is the London penetration depth \cite{Mueller2019}. It defines how deep the magnetic field will penetrate into the superconductor. 
\subsection{Types of superconductors}
Not all superconductors have the same properties, however. Superconductors can be divided into type-$\RNum{1}$ and type-$\RNum{2}$ superconductors. Type-$\RNum{1}$ superconductors are  superconductors that exhibit the Meißner effect until its critical field $B_c$. Comparatively, a type-$\RNum{2}$ superconductor has 2 critical fields, $B_{c1}$ and $B_{c2}$. Until reaching the first critical field $B_{c1}$, a type-$\RNum{2}$ superconductor display the same properties as a type-$\RNum{1}$ superconductor. Between $B_{c1}$ and $B_{c2}$, however, type-$\RNum{2}$ superconductors create canals that let flux penetrate into the material. Each canal, also called vortices, allows exactly one quantum flux $\Phi$. The vortices arrange themselves in a triangular lattice, called the Abrikosov-lattice \cite{Mueller2019}. The different behavior between type-$\RNum{1}$ and type-$\RNum{2}$ superconductors can be understood when taking a look at the Ginzburg-Landau parameter $\kappa$:
\begin{equation}
    \kappa = \frac{\lambda_{L}}{\xi_{GL}}
\end{equation}

where $\xi_{GL}$ is the Ginzburg-Landau coherence length, which determines how quickly the cooper-pair density increases near the surface than deep in the material. When $\kappa < \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$, the superconductor behaves like a type-$\RNum{1}$ superconductor, and if $\kappa > \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$, the superconductor behaves like a type-$\RNum{2}$ superconductor. \\ \\
Intuitively, this can be understood as a fight between two opposing effects: A superconductor wants to maximize its surface to minimize the amount of energy needed to block magnetic flux from going into the material. At the same type, surfaces reduce the condensation energy, since there are less Cooper-pairs near the surface. For type-$\RNum{2}$ superconductors, it is more favorable to create small vortices and lose the condensation energy from Cooper-pairs, but save on the expulsion energy against the magnetic field \cite{Hunklinger2018}.

\section{Photolithography}
Photolithography or optical lithography refers to the process of creating structures on a sample by applying a photosensitive polymer material called photoresist onto it and exposing it to light, often in the ultraviolet wavelengths \cite{Mack2006LithographyOnline}. Typically, a lithography process involves applying the photoresist onto the sample via spin coating, heating it (also called prebake), applying a mask, and exposing the sample to UV light. Masks can be omitted by using maskless aligners (MLA), which are used in our lab as well. The exposed parts of the photoresist either become more soluble (positive photoresist) or less soluble (negative photoresist) to a solution called developer, which is applied after the light exposure \cite{AllresistPhotoresistOnline}. Developing the sample washes the soluble part off, creating structure on the sample.  
\subsection{Cross-linking}
Negative photoresists achieve high chemical resistance upon exposure by a process called cross-linking. Cross-linking refers to the reaction where individual polymers chain together to build a single, large polymer, changing the property of the material. In the $\textit{AR-N 1407.17 new}$ negative photoresist, which was used in our experiments, this is done by radical starters that form reactive radicals upon exposure to UV light which starts a chain reaction connecting individual polymers together.
