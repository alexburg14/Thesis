\section{Fundamentals of Magnetism}
\subsection{Macrospin Model}
The magnetic properties of a ferromagnet without anisotropy can be best described by treating the ferromagnet as having one average magnetic dipole. This average dipole, called Magnetization $\boldsymbol{M}$, can then be calculated by the sum of all dipole moments $\mu$ of a sample:
\begin{equation} 
\boldsymbol{M} = \frac{1}{V}\sum_{i\in V}{\boldsymbol{\mu_i}}
\end{equation}
From classical electrodynamics, we know that the magnetic dipoles $\mu$ are related to the angular momentum $\textbf{L}$ by the gyromagnetic ratio $\gamma$, due to the electron creating a current through its orbital path:
\begin{equation}
   \boldsymbol{\mu} = \gamma \textbf{L} 
   \label{Drehimpulsgleichung}
\end{equation}
Furthermore, when an external field is applied to a magnetic dipole moment $\mu$, it causes a torque $\textbf{T}$ on the magnetic moment:
\begin{equation}
    \textbf{T} = \boldsymbol{\mu} \times \textbf{B}
    \label{Drehmoment von dipolmoment}
\end{equation}
By using equations \ref{Drehimpulsgleichung} and \ref{Drehmoment von dipolmoment}, as well as the fact that $\textbf{T} = \diff{\textbf{L}}{t}$, we get the following equation of motion for a dipole moment $\boldsymbol{\mu}$:
\begin{equation}
    \diff{\boldsymbol{\mu}}{t} = \gamma \boldsymbol{\mu} \times \textbf{B} 
    \label{EOM fuer dipolmoment}
\end{equation}
Inside a magnetized medium, the magnetic flux density takes on the following form \cite{obstbaum2014}:
\begin{equation} 
\textbf{B} = \mu_0(\textbf{H+M}),
\end{equation}
where $\textbf{H}$ is the magnetic field applied.
To transfer this result to the dynamics of the magnetization of a medium, equation (\ref{EOM fuer dipolmoment}) has to be reformulated in such a way that it accounts for the effective field $\boldsymbol{{H_{\text{eff}}}}$ inside the medium. Doing this, we get the Landau-Lifschitz equation \cite{obstbaum2014}:
\begin{equation}
    \diff{\textbf{M}}{t} = - \gamma \textbf{M} \times \mu_0 \boldsymbol{{H_{\text{eff}}}}
    \label{Landau-Lifschitz}
\end{equation}
Here, $\boldsymbol{{H_{\text{eff}}}}$ combines all possible field terms inside the medium, like anisotropy $\boldsymbol{H_{\text{ani}}}$, exchange interaction $\boldsymbol{H_{\text{ex}}}$, and the demagnetizing field $\boldsymbol{H_{\text{dem}}}$ \cite{obstbaum2014}.
Solving this differential equation leads to a magnetization undergoing precession around the effective field with the frequency $ \omega = \gamma \mu_0 H_{\text{eff}}$ \cite{Mueller2019}, as shown in figure \ref{fig:precession picture}.

As stated above, the Landau-Lifschitz equation (\ref{Landau-Lifschitz}) describes a perpetual precession around the effective magnetic field. In a real physical system, however, the precession amplitude declines due to various dissipation effects such as magnon-phonon relaxation or radiative damping. Gilbert therefore introduced a damping term with the material-dependent coefficient $\alpha$ with which we get the Landau-Lifschitz-Gilbert (LLG) equation \cite{Liensberger2021}
\begin{equation}
    \diff{\textbf{M}}{t} = - \gamma \textbf{M} \times \mu_0 \boldsymbol{{H_{\text{eff}}}} + \frac{\alpha}{M_s}\boldsymbol{M} \times\diff{\textbf{M}}{t},
\end{equation}
where $M_s$ refers to the saturation magnetization. 

\subsection{Ferromagnetic Resonance}
In order to investigate the resonance condition of this precession, we introduce an oscillating magnetic field $\boldsymbol{h_{\text{rf}}}$ that is perpendicular to $\boldsymbol{{H_{\text{eff}}}}$. $\boldsymbol{h_{\text{rf}}}$ can generate a torque that can compensate the damping of the precession when the frequency of the precession of the magnetization and the frequency of the oscillating field match, which can be compared to a driven harmonic oscillator in the classical view \cite{Liensberger2021}.
The following derivation will closely follow references \cite{Mueller2019} and \cite{Liensberger2017}. We choose our coordinate system so that the external magnetic field $\boldsymbol{{H_{\text{ext}}}}$ is applied in the z-axis and $\boldsymbol{h_{\text{rf}}}$ rotates in the xy-plane. We split $\textbf{H}$ and $\textbf{M}$ into their respective time-independent and time-dependent parts
\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
        \mathbf{M} &= \mathbf{M}_0 + \mathbf{m}(t) = M_0 \mathbf{\hat{e}}_z + \mathbf{m}(t) \\
        \mathbf{H}_{\text{eff}} &= \mathbf{H}_{\text{ext}} - \mathbf{H}_{\text{dem}} + \mathbf{h}_{\text{rf}}(t) \\
        &= (H_{\text{ext}} - N_z M_0) \mathbf{\hat{e}}_z - N_x m_x(t) \mathbf{\hat{e}}_x - N_y m_y(t) \mathbf{\hat{e}}_y + \mathbf{h}_{\text{rf}}(t) 
    \end{aligned}
    \label{time-dependent and independent}
\end{equation}
where $N_{x/y/z}$ denote the terms from the demagnetization tensor. For simplicity, the anisotropy field is neglected.

\begin{equation}
\omega_\text{res} = \gamma \mu_0 \sqrt{H_{\text{ext}}(H_{\text{ext}}+M_\text{s})}
\end{equation}
Additionally, the derivation gives us an equation for the resonance line width $\Delta H_{FWHM}$, as a function of the driving-frequency and the damping coefficient \cite{Mueller2023}:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta H_{FWHM} = 2 \frac{\alpha \omega}{\mu_0 \gamma}
    \label{linewidth equation}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha$ is the Gilbert-zdamping coefficient and $\Delta H_{FWHM}$ the line width defined as the full-width at half-maximum.

When a ferromagnetic film comes in contact with a normal metal, an increase in damping is observed, which is known as spin pumping. Theoretically, this can be explained by a pure spin current $\boldsymbol{J_\textbf{s}}$ that gets sent across the ferromagnet/metal interface which transports angular momentum from the ferromagnet to the normal metal, hence increasing the damping. The spin current density is given by  \cite{Mueller2023}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{J}_{\mathbf{s}}=\frac{\hbar}{4 \pi}\left(\operatorname{Re}\left(G_{\uparrow \downarrow}\right) \mathbf{M} \times \frac{d \mathbf{M}}{d t}-\operatorname{Im}\left(G_{\uparrow \downarrow}\right) \frac{d \mathbf{M}}{d t}\right)
\end{equation}
where $G_{\uparrow \downarrow}$ is the spin-mixing conductance that describes the transport of spins that are not collinear to the magnetization of the sample. \section{Fundamentals of Superconductivity}
In this section, the fundamental theory behind superconductivity is discussed that is needed for the discussion of the results given in chapter 5 and 6. 

Superconductors are materials that exhibit two distinct properties below a certain temperature $T_\textrm{C}$: The vanishing of electrical resistance and the expulsion of the magnetic field inside the material, also called the Meißner effect. In its essence, superconductivity is caused by a positive attraction of two electrons through an exchange of virtual phonons. Intuitively, this can be explained by an electron moving through the crystal, causing a deformation in the lattice that generates a positive dipole moment, which in turns attracts another electron. These so-called Cooper-pairs have opposite momentum $\textbf{k}$ and $\textbf{-k}$  WAS GENAU MEINST DU HIER?\cite{Hunklinger2018}. 

\subsection{The London Equations}
In 1935, two brothers developed the London-equations in an attempt to phenomenologically explain the behavior of superconductors:
HERLEITUNG
\begin{align}
    \frac{\partial\boldsymbol{j_s}}{\partial t} \ =& \  \frac{ne^2}{m}\textbf{E} \\
    \nabla \times \boldsymbol{j_s} \ =& \ -\frac{ne^2}{m}\textbf{B}
\end{align}
where $n$ represents the Cooper-pair density and $m$ the mass of a Cooper-pair.
Solving these equations yields 
\begin{align}
    \textbf{B(}\textbf{r}) =& \boldsymbol{B_0}e^{-\frac{|r|}{\lambda_L}},
\end{align}
where $\lambda_L$ = $\frac{m}{\mu_0 ne^2}$ is the London penetration depth \cite{Mueller2019}. It measures how deep an magnetic field will penetrate into the superconductor. 
\subsection{Types of superconductors}
Not all superconductors(SCs) have the same properties, however. SCs can be divided into type-$\RNum{1}$ and type-$\RNum{2}$ SCs. Type-$\RNum{1}$ SCs are  SCs that exhibit the Meißner effect below their critical field $B_\textrm{C}$. Comparatively, a type-$\RNum{2}$ SC has two critical fields, $B_{\textrm{C1}}$ and $B_{\textrm{C2}}$ . Until reaching the first critical field $B_{\textrm{C1}}$, a type-$\RNum{2}$ SC displays the same properties as a type-$\RNum{1}$ SC. Between $B_{\textrm{C1}}$  and $B_{\textrm{C2}}$, however, type-$\RNum{2}$ SCs create canals that let flux penetrate into the material. Each canal, also called vortex, allows exactly one quantum flux $\Phi$. The vortices arrange themselves in a triangular lattice, called the Abrikosov-lattice \cite{Mueller2019}. The different behavior between type-$\RNum{1}$ and type-$\RNum{2}$ SCs can be understood when taking a look at the Ginzburg-Landau parameter $\kappa$:
\begin{equation}
    \kappa = \frac{\lambda_{L}}{\xi_{GL}}
\end{equation}

where $\xi_{GL}$ is the Ginzburg-Landau coherence length, which determines how quickly the cooper-pair density increases near the surface. When $\kappa < \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$, the superconductor behaves like a type-$\RNum{1}$ superconductor, and if $\kappa > \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$, the superconductor is a type-$\RNum{2}$ superconductor. 
Intuitively, this can be understood as a fight between two opposing effects: Maximizing surface area minimizes the amount of energy needed to expel magnetic flux from going into the material. At the same time, surfaces reduce the gain of condensation energy, since there are less Cooper-pairs near the surface. For type-$\RNum{2}$ superconductors, it is more favorable to create small vortices and which costs condensation energy from Cooper-pairs, but save on the expulsion energy against the magnetic field \cite{Hunklinger2018}.

\section{Photolithography}
Photolithography, or optical lithography, refers to the process of creating structures on a sample by applying a photosensitive polymer material called photoresist and then exposing it to light, often in the ultraviolet wavelengths \cite{Mack2006LithographyOnline}. In this thesis, optical lithography is used to form structure on the sample and to create an unremovable isolation layer.

\subsection{Typical photolithography process}
Typically, a lithography process involves applying the photoresist onto the sample via spin coating, heating it (also called prebake), applying a mask, and exposing the photoresist to UV light. Masks can be omitted by using maskless aligners (MLA), which exposes the photoresist locally by a focused laser. Light exposure changes the solubility of the photoresist: exposed regions of a positive photoresist become more soluble in the developer, while those of a negative photoresist become less soluble \cite{AllresistPhotoresistOnline}. Developing the sample washes the soluble part off, creating a pattern on the sample that can be used to etch away specific parts of the sample, thus fabricating structures. Finally, the remaining photoresist can be washed away with a remover. A sample illustration can be seen in figure \ref{fig:photolithography}.

\subsection{Cross-linking}
Negative photoresists achieve high chemical resistance upon exposure by a process called cross-linking. Cross-linking refers to the reaction where individual polymers chain together to build a single, large polymer, changing the property of the material \cite{AllresistPhotoresistOnline}. In the $\textit{AR-N 1407.17 new}$ (AR-N) negative photoresist, which was used in our experiments, this is done by radical starters that form reactive radicals upon exposure to UV light or high energy electrons, which starts a chain reaction connecting individual polymers together.
